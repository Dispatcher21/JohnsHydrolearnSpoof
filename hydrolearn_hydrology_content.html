Learning Activity: Unit Hydrograph, Rational Method, Curve Number & Hydrologic Routing

This learning activity provides comprehensive coverage of four core hydrologic methods used to estimate runoff from rainfall. By the end of this activity you should be able to identify key terminology, read and interpret Unit Hydrograph figures, apply the Rational Method and Curve Number procedure to calculate peak flow and runoff volume, and describe the process of hydrologic routing.

Section 1: Unit Hydrograph (UH) Theory

1.1 What is a Unit Hydrograph?

A Unit Hydrograph (UH) is the direct runoff hydrograph that results from one unit (typically 1 inch or 1 cm) of excess rainfall (direct runoff) generated uniformly over a watershed at a uniform rate during a specified duration. It is one of the most widely used tools in engineering hydrology for transforming rainfall excess into a runoff hydrograph.

Core Concept
The UH is a linear model. It assumes that the watershed response to rainfall is proportional and time-invariant — meaning a 2-inch storm produces exactly twice the runoff of a 1-inch storm, with the same shape.

1.2 Key Assumptions of Unit Hydrograph Theory

  • Linearity: Runoff is directly proportional to rainfall excess. Doubling the rainfall excess doubles all ordinates of the hydrograph.
  • Time-invariance: The watershed responds the same way regardless of when the storm occurs (i.e., the UH shape does not change over time).
  • Uniform rainfall: Rainfall excess is assumed to occur uniformly over the entire watershed for the storm duration.
  • Uniform spatial distribution: Rainfall is distributed uniformly in space across the watershed.
  • Cause–effect relationship: All runoff is attributed only to the rainfall event; base flow is separated and handled separately.

1.3 Essential Unit Hydrograph Terminology

The following terms appear on every UH figure and must be clearly understood:

Rainfall Excess (Effective Rainfall)Pe

The portion of total rainfall that becomes direct surface runoff after all abstractions (infiltration, depression storage, evaporation) have been removed. It is the "net" rainfall that generates runoff.

Units: inches or mm; for a UH this equals exactly 1 in or 1 cm
Direct Runoff Hydrograph (DRH)

A hydrograph of streamflow that has had the base flow component removed. It represents only the runoff directly generated by a rainfall event. The DRH is the output of applying a UH to a rainfall excess.

Base Flow

The sustained, slow contribution to streamflow from groundwater (subsurface) sources. It is the background streamflow present before, during, and after a storm event. Base flow must be separated from the total hydrograph before deriving a UH.

Peak Flow / Peak DischargeQp

The maximum instantaneous rate of flow that occurs during a runoff event. For a UH, the peak flow represents the highest ordinate on the hydrograph.

Units: cfs (ft³/s) or m³/s
Time to Peaktp

The time from the beginning of rainfall excess (or the centroid of rainfall excess) to the peak discharge of the hydrograph. Also called the time of concentration in some formulations, though these are technically distinct.

Units: hours or minutes
Time of Concentrationtc

The time required for water to travel from the hydraulically most distant point in the watershed to the outlet. When tc is exceeded, the entire watershed is contributing to runoff at the outlet. It is a fundamental characteristic of a watershed's response speed.

Units: hours or minutes
Lag TimetL

The time from the centroid of the rainfall excess to the peak of the direct runoff hydrograph. Lag time is related to but distinct from time of concentration. A common approximation: tL ≈ 0.6 tc.

Units: hours
Storm Duration (UH Duration)tr

The duration of the rainfall excess that produced the UH. A UH is always associated with a specific storm duration — a 1-hour UH was derived from (or applies to) a storm with rainfall excess spread over 1 hour.

Units: hours
Runoff VolumeVr

The total volume of direct runoff produced by the storm. On a hydrograph, this is the area under the direct runoff hydrograph curve. For a UH with 1 inch of rainfall excess over drainage area A: V = 1 in × A.

Units: acre-feet, ft³, or m³
Hydrograph Recession Limb

The falling portion of the hydrograph after the peak, representing the drainage of water stored in the watershed (channel storage, overland flow) as the watershed empties. The shape of the recession is often modeled exponentially.

Rising Limb

The portion of the hydrograph from the start of runoff to the peak. It reflects the rapid increase in discharge as rainfall excess reaches the outlet. The rising limb is typically steeper than the recession limb.

S-Curve (S-Hydrograph)

A hydrograph produced by an indefinitely long series of unit storms. It is used to derive a UH of any duration from a UH of a different duration through a mathematical lag-and-difference operation.

Synthetic Unit Hydrograph

A UH derived from watershed physical characteristics (area, slope, length) rather than from measured streamflow data. Common synthetic UH methods include the SCS (NRCS) Dimensionless Unit Hydrograph and Snyder's method. Used when no streamflow records exist.

Convolution

The mathematical process of multiplying and summing UH ordinates by rainfall excess depths to obtain the total direct runoff hydrograph from a multi-period storm. This is the primary application of the UH in practice.

A standard UH figure contains the following elements. You must be able to extract key values from it:

Common Exam Mistake
Do not confuse the UH ordinate (discharge per inch of rainfall) with actual discharge. To get actual discharge from a storm, you must multiply the UH ordinate by the actual rainfall excess depth (in inches or cm).

1.5 SCS Triangular Unit Hydrograph

The NRCS (formerly SCS) Triangular UH simplifies the runoff hydrograph to a triangle. Its key relationships are:

SCS Triangular UH Equations

Time to peak:    tp = tr/2 + tL    where tL = 0.6 tc

Peak discharge:    Qp = (484 × A × Q) / tp

     A = drainage area (mi²), Q = runoff depth (in), tp = time to peak (hr)

Time base:    Tb = 2.67 × tp

The constant 484 (sometimes written as 0.484 with different units) is the peaking factor. It reflects the relative steepness of the rising vs. recession limbs. A value of 484 corresponds to conditions where the recession limb is 1.67× longer than the rising limb.

1.6 Answering UH Questions: Peak, Time, and Volume

Finding Peak Runoff from a UH

Given a UH and a rainfall excess depth Pe:

Peak Discharge from UH
Qpeak, actual = Qp,UH × Pe

where Qp,UH is the UH peak ordinate (cfs/in) and Pe is rainfall excess (in)

Finding Time to Peak

Time to peak is read directly from the UH figure as the x-coordinate of the maximum y-value. It does not change when you scale the UH for a different rainfall depth.

Finding Runoff Volume

For a UH producing 1 inch of runoff over area A:

Runoff Volume
V = Pe × A      (must use consistent units)

Convert: 1 inch over 1 acre = 1/12 ft³ × 43,560 ft²/acre = 3,630 ft³ = 1 acre-inch

Or, integrate the DRH: V = Σ(Qi × Δt)    (sum of ordinates × time step)

Worked Example 1: Reading a UH

Given: A 2-hour UH for a 150-acre watershed has a peak ordinate of 320 cfs/in at t = 3 hours. A storm produces 2.5 inches of rainfall excess in 2 hours.

Step 1 — Find peak discharge:
Qpeak = 320 cfs/in × 2.5 in = 800 cfs
Step 2 — Identify time to peak:
tp = 3 hours (read directly from the UH figure — unchanged regardless of storm depth)
Step 3 — Find runoff volume:
V = 2.5 in × 150 acres = 375 acre-inches = 375 × 3,630 ft³ = 1,361,250 ft³
Peak = 800 cfs  |  tp = 3 hr  |  Volume = 375 acre-in

1.7 Convolution: Applying a UH to a Multi-Period Storm

When a storm has multiple time increments of rainfall excess (P1, P2, P3, ...), the total DRH is obtained by convolving the UH with each increment and summing:

  1. Multiply all UH ordinates by P1; record starting at time 0.
  2. Multiply all UH ordinates by P2; record starting at time Δt (one time step later).
  3. Repeat for each rainfall excess increment, shifting by Δt each time.
  4. Sum all lagged hydrographs at each time step to get the total DRH.
  5. Add base flow back to the DRH to obtain the total stream hydrograph.
Key Rule
Each rainfall excess block is associated with its own copy of the UH, shifted in time by the block's start time. Linearity makes the sum of the individual contributions equal the total watershed response.

Section 2: The Rational Method

2.1 Overview and Applicability

The Rational Method is one of the oldest and most widely used methods in urban stormwater hydrology for estimating the peak discharge from a small drainage area. It is most appropriate for:

  • Urban and suburban catchments with drainage areas typically less than 200 acres (80 ha) — some references extend to 640 acres (1 mi²)
  • Areas with relatively homogeneous land cover
  • Storm drain design, culvert sizing, and inlet design
  • Areas where no runoff data exist
Limitation
The Rational Method only estimates peak discharge — it does not produce a hydrograph. For volume-based calculations or hydrograph shape, use the UH or CN method instead.

2.2 The Rational Formula

The Rational Method Equation

Q = C × i × A

  • Q = Peak discharge (ft³/s = cfs in US customary; m³/s in SI with conversion)
  • C = Runoff coefficient (dimensionless, 0 to 1.0)
  • i = Rainfall intensity (in/hr for US customary)
  • A = Drainage area (acres for US customary)

Note: In US customary units with A in acres and i in in/hr, Q is directly in cfs (the unit conversion factor is very close to 1.008 and is typically omitted).

2.3 The Runoff Coefficient (C)

The runoff coefficient represents the fraction of rainfall that becomes surface runoff. It accounts for the combined effect of infiltration, depression storage, and other losses. C ranges from 0 (all rainfall infiltrates) to 1.0 (all rainfall runs off).

Composite Runoff Coefficient

When a watershed contains multiple land cover types, use an area-weighted composite C:

Composite Runoff Coefficient
Ccomposite = (C1×A1 + C2×A2 + ... + Cn×An) / Atotal

2.4 Rainfall Intensity (i)

Rainfall intensity in the Rational Method is selected based on two criteria:

  1. Storm duration = Time of Concentration (tc): The design storm duration is set equal to the time of concentration of the watershed. This assumption maximizes the peak flow because the entire watershed is contributing simultaneously.
  2. Return period: Select the design storm recurrence interval appropriate for the structure (e.g., 10-year storm for a storm drain inlet, 100-year storm for a major culvert).

Intensity values are obtained from Intensity-Duration-Frequency (IDF) curves, which are regional rainfall relationships showing rainfall intensity as a function of storm duration and return period. IDF curves are published by NOAA and local drainage authorities.

How to Use an IDF Curve
1. Calculate tc for your watershed.
2. Select your design return period (2-yr, 10-yr, 25-yr, 100-yr, etc.).
3. Enter the IDF curve at duration = tc.
4. Read off the intensity in in/hr.
5. Use this i value in Q = CiA.

2.5 Time of Concentration (tc)

Several methods exist to estimate tc. Common approaches include:

  • Kirpich equation: tc = 0.0078 × L0.77 × S-0.385   (t in min, L in ft, S = slope ft/ft)
  • NRCS Lag method: tc = tL / 0.6, where tL is computed from CN, L, and Y
  • Sheet flow + shallow concentrated flow + channel flow: The total tc is the sum of travel times through each flow segment.

2.6 Worked Examples — Rational Method

Worked Example 2: Basic Rational Method Calculation

Given: A 12-acre urban watershed. Land cover: 40% impervious pavement (C = 0.85), 60% residential lawn (C = 0.30). Time of concentration = 20 minutes. Design storm: 10-year return period; IDF curve gives i = 4.2 in/hr for 20-min duration.

Step 1 — Composite C:
C = (0.85 × 0.40 × 12 + 0.30 × 0.60 × 12) / 12 = (4.08 + 2.16) / 12 = 6.24/12 = 0.52
Step 2 — Apply Q = CiA:
Q = 0.52 × 4.2 in/hr × 12 acres = 26.2 cfs
Design peak discharge Q = 26.2 cfs
Worked Example 3: Solving for Drainage Area

Given: A storm drain inlet must be sized to handle Q = 10 cfs. C = 0.75, i = 3.5 in/hr. What is the maximum contributing drainage area?

Rearrange Q = CiA:
A = Q / (C × i) = 10 / (0.75 × 3.5) = 10 / 2.625 = 3.81 acres
Maximum drainage area = 3.81 acres

2.7 Key Concepts and Common Questions

  • Why set duration = tc? If the storm duration is shorter than tc, only part of the watershed contributes at the outlet. If longer, intensity decreases (on IDF curve). The critical condition (maximum Q) occurs at duration = tc.
  • Does the Rational Method give volume? No. It gives peak discharge only. Volume would require duration × peak, but that overestimates because the hydrograph is not rectangular.
  • What happens to Q if impervious area increases? C increases, so Q increases proportionally — this is why urbanization increases flooding.
  • Modified Rational Method: An extension that produces a trapezoidal runoff hydrograph, allowing volume estimation. The rising limb goes from 0 to Qpeak over tc, holds at Qpeak for the storm duration, then recedes over tc.

Section 3: The Curve Number (CN) Method

3.1 Background and Purpose

The NRCS Curve Number (CN) method, developed by the Natural Resources Conservation Service (formerly the Soil Conservation Service), is a widely used empirical procedure for estimating runoff volume from a storm of known total rainfall depth. Unlike the Rational Method, the CN method accounts for antecedent soil moisture conditions, soil type, and land cover through a single dimensionless parameter: the Curve Number.

The CN method is commonly used for:

  • Computing runoff volume for storm events
  • Calculating rainfall excess for input to Unit Hydrograph convolution
  • Comparing pre- vs. post-development runoff conditions
  • Regional and ungaged watershed analysis

3.2 Key CN Terminology

Curve Number (CN)

A dimensionless index (0–100) that represents the runoff potential of a watershed based on its soils, land cover, and antecedent moisture condition. CN = 100 means 100% runoff (impervious); CN approaching 0 means essentially no runoff.

Dimensionless; typical range: 30–98
Potential Maximum Retention (S)

The maximum depth of water that the soil-cover complex can retain (store) after runoff begins. It is computed directly from CN and represents the total available storage in the watershed beyond the initial abstraction.

Units: inches
Initial Abstraction (Ia)

The total rainfall depth that must fall before any runoff begins. It includes interception by vegetation, filling of surface depressions, and initial infiltration. The standard NRCS approximation is Ia = 0.2S.

Units: inches
Hydrologic Soil Groups (HSG)

Soils are classified into four groups (A, B, C, D) based on their minimum infiltration rate and runoff potential. Group A has the highest infiltration (lowest runoff); Group D has the lowest infiltration (highest runoff).

  • Group A: Sand, loamy sand — High infiltration, low runoff. CN ~30–60.
  • Group B: Silt loam, loam — Moderate infiltration. CN ~55–75.
  • Group C: Sandy clay loam — Slow infiltration. CN ~70–85.
  • Group D: Clay, clay loam — Very slow infiltration, high runoff. CN ~80–98.
Antecedent Moisture Condition (AMC)

The soil moisture status before a storm, which affects how much rainfall the soil can absorb. Three conditions are defined: AMC I (dry), AMC II (average/normal — the standard condition for published CN tables), and AMC III (wet/saturated). CN values in standard tables are for AMC II.

3.3 The CN Equations

Step 1 — Potential Maximum Retention from CN
S = (1000 / CN) − 10      (S in inches, valid for CN > 0)
Step 2 — Initial Abstraction
Ia = 0.2 × S
Step 3 — Runoff Depth

If P > Ia:

Q = (P − Ia)² / (P − Ia + S)

If P ≤ Ia:    Q = 0 (no runoff)

where P = total rainfall depth (in), Q = direct runoff depth (in), Ia = initial abstraction (in), S = potential maximum retention (in)

Step 4 — Runoff Volume
V = Q × A      (volume = runoff depth × watershed area)

Convert: if Q in inches and A in acres, V = Q × A in acre-inches; × 3,630 for ft³; × 0.0826 for acre-feet

Simplified Form
Substituting Ia = 0.2S into the runoff equation:

Q = (P − 0.2S)² / (P + 0.8S)

This is the most commonly used form in practice.

3.5 Computing the Composite (Average) CN

When a watershed contains multiple land use/soil type combinations, compute an area-weighted average CN:

Area-Weighted Composite CN
CNcomposite = (CN1×A1 + CN2×A2 + ... + CNn×An) / Atotal

Round the final CN to the nearest whole number.

Important Note
Always compute the composite CN before computing S. Do not compute S for each sub-area and then average — this gives a different (incorrect) result.

3.6 Worked Examples — Curve Number

Worked Example 4: Computing Composite CN and Runoff Volume

Given: A 500-acre watershed with the following sub-areas (all HSG B soils):

  • 150 acres of residential ½-acre lots (CN = 70)
  • 200 acres of commercial area (CN = 92)
  • 150 acres of parks/open space, good condition (CN = 61)

A 24-hour storm produces P = 4.0 inches of total rainfall. Compute runoff depth and volume.

Step 1 — Composite CN:
CN = (70×150 + 92×200 + 61×150) / 500
CN = (10,500 + 18,400 + 9,150) / 500 = 38,050 / 500 = 76.1 → use CN = 76
Step 2 — Compute S:
S = (1000/76) − 10 = 13.16 − 10 = 3.16 in
Step 3 — Initial Abstraction:
Ia = 0.2 × 3.16 = 0.63 in
Since P = 4.0 in > Ia = 0.63 in → runoff occurs
Step 4 — Runoff Depth:
Q = (4.0 − 0.63)² / (4.0 − 0.63 + 3.16) = (3.37)² / (6.53) = 11.36 / 6.53 = 1.74 in
Step 5 — Runoff Volume:
V = 1.74 in × 500 acres = 870 acre-inches
V = 870 × 3,630 ft³ = 3,158,100 ft³ = 72.5 acre-feet
CN = 76 | Q = 1.74 in | Volume = 870 acre-in (72.5 ac-ft)
Worked Example 5: Effect of Urbanization on Runoff

Pre-development: 200 acres of meadow, HSG B (CN = 58).   Post-development: 200 acres converted to residential ⅛-acre lots, HSG B (CN = 85). Storm: P = 3.5 in.

Pre-development: S = 1000/58 − 10 = 7.24 in; Ia = 1.45 in; P > Ia: Q = (3.5−1.45)²/(3.5−1.45+7.24) = (2.05)²/9.29 = 4.20/9.29 = 0.45 in
Post-development: S = 1000/85 − 10 = 1.76 in; Ia = 0.35 in; Q = (3.5−0.35)²/(3.5−0.35+1.76) = (3.15)²/4.91 = 9.92/4.91 = 2.02 in
Urbanization increases runoff depth from 0.45 in to 2.02 in — a 4.5× increase in runoff!

3.7 Relationship between CN and Rational Method C

While both C and CN describe runoff potential, they are not directly equivalent. CN is a storm-event-averaged measure of abstraction; C is a peak-rate coefficient. CN gives runoff volume; C gives peak rate. For large impervious areas, both approach their maximum values (CN ≈ 98, C ≈ 0.95), but they diverge significantly for pervious land covers.

Section 4: Hydrologic Routing

4.1 What is Hydrologic Routing?

Hydrologic routing is the procedure used to predict the temporal and spatial variation of a flood wave as it travels through a river reach, reservoir, or detention basin. In simpler terms, it takes an inflow hydrograph at one location and transforms it into an outflow hydrograph at a downstream location, accounting for the storage effects of the reach or basin along the way.

Why is Routing Necessary?
A hydrograph produced at one point in a watershed (say, from a UH analysis) will not arrive at a downstream point unchanged. The channel or basin stores water, which attenuates (reduces) the peak and lags (delays) the timing of the peak. Routing quantifies these effects.

4.2 The Continuity Equation: Foundation of Routing

All hydrologic routing methods are based on the continuity equation (conservation of mass):

Continuity Equation for Routing

I − O = dS/dt

  • I = Inflow rate into the reach or basin (cfs or m³/s)
  • O = Outflow rate from the reach or basin (cfs or m³/s)
  • dS/dt = Rate of change of storage within the system (ft³/s or m³/s)

In finite difference form over a time step Δt:

(I1 + I2)/2 − (O1 + O2)/2 = (S2 − S1) / Δt

4.3 Types of Hydrologic Routing

There are two primary categories of routing, distinguished by where the routing takes place:

Reservoir (Level-Pool) Routing

Reservoir routing (also called level-pool routing or storage routing) is used to route inflow through a pond, lake, detention basin, or reservoir. Key characteristics:

  • Assumes the water surface in the storage element is always horizontal (level pool).
  • Outflow is a unique function of storage: O = f(S), derived from stage-storage and stage-discharge relationships.
  • The storage indication method (Modified Puls method) is the most common computational approach.
  • The routing consistently attenuates and delays the peak: the outflow peak is lower and occurs later than the inflow peak.
Storage Indication Method (Modified Puls)
Rearrange continuity equation to isolate unknowns on the right side:

(I1 + I2) + (2S1/Δt − O1) = (2S2/Δt + O2)

Build a routing curve of (2S/Δt + O) vs. O, then step through time using the known inflow hydrograph.

Channel (Stream) Routing

Channel routing routes a flood wave through a river reach. Unlike reservoir routing, the water surface is not horizontal — the wave moves progressively downstream. The most widely used hydrologic channel routing method is the Muskingum method.

Muskingum Method Storage Equation
S = K [x·I + (1−x)·O]
  • K = travel time of the flood wave through the reach (hours) — a watershed routing parameter
  • x = weighting factor (dimensionless, 0 ≤ x ≤ 0.5) — describes relative influence of inflow vs. outflow on storage; x = 0 is pure reservoir routing, x = 0.5 is pure translation (no attenuation)

Outflow at next time step: O2 = C0I2 + C1I1 + C2O1

where C0, C1, C2 are routing coefficients computed from K, x, and Δt; must sum to 1.0.

4.4 Key Concepts and Effects of Routing

4.5 The Routing Process Step-by-Step

The general process for performing hydrologic routing is:

  1. Define the system: Identify whether you are routing through a reservoir/detention basin (level-pool) or a stream reach (Muskingum).
  2. Establish storage relationships:
    • For reservoirs: develop stage-storage curve (elevation vs. volume) and stage-discharge curve (elevation vs. outflow through spillway/outlet).
    • For channels: estimate K and x from field data or calibration.
  3. Select time step Δt: Must be short enough to capture the shape of the inflow hydrograph — typically Δt ≤ tp/5 and Δt ≤ K for Muskingum routing.
  4. Apply the continuity equation: Step through the inflow hydrograph one time interval at a time, solving for outflow at each step using the appropriate method.
  5. Check volume balance: Verify that total inflow volume ≈ total outflow volume + change in storage to confirm no numerical errors.
  6. Interpret results: Compare inflow and outflow peak, time of peak, and total volume. Evaluate whether design criteria (e.g., peak reduction of 70%) are met.

4.6 Applications of Routing in Design

  • Detention basin design: Size a pond to reduce post-development peak flow to pre-development levels. Route the post-development inflow hydrograph through the proposed basin and adjust the outlet structure until the outflow peak meets the target.
  • Flood forecasting: Route upstream observed hydrographs downstream through river reaches to forecast arrival time and peak at downstream points.
  • Dam spillway design: Route the probable maximum flood (PMF) through a reservoir to determine the required spillway capacity.
  • Green infrastructure evaluation: Route runoff through bioretention cells, wet ponds, and constructed wetlands to assess peak reduction and water quality benefits.
Summary: What Routing Tells Us
Routing answers the question: "Given that this hydrograph enters a reach or basin, what hydrograph comes out the other end?" It is essential for sizing detention ponds, assessing downstream flood impacts, and evaluating green infrastructure effectiveness. The two fundamental effects are always attenuation (lower peak) and lag (delayed peak), while total volume is conserved.

Summary: Choosing the Right Method

Practical Workflow
A typical complete design sequence combines all four methods:
  1. Use CN method to compute runoff depth (rainfall excess) from a design storm.
  2. Apply Unit Hydrograph convolution to transform rainfall excess into a runoff hydrograph at the watershed outlet.
  3. Use Hydrologic Routing to route that hydrograph through a proposed detention basin to find the attenuated outflow.
  4. For quick peak estimates on small urban areas, use the Rational Method directly.
